# Metaphor Mapping

Metaphor exploration is the most important step in the naming process. It's where you move from "what does this product do?" to "what else in the world works like this?" — and that shift produces names that are concrete, visual, and memorable.

---

## The Technique

### Step 1: Define the Core Function

Write one sentence: "This product [does what] for [whom]."

Don't describe features. Describe the essential function.
- Not: "This product aggregates RSS feeds, filters by keyword, enriches with AI summaries, and delivers to Telegram"
- But: "This product watches the web and delivers relevant news to you"

The simplified version reveals metaphor territory. "Watches" opens up sentries, lookouts, radar, owls. "Delivers" opens up couriers, heralds, mailmen. "Watches the web" is a spider, a net, a trawler.

### Step 2: Ask Metaphor-Finding Questions

For your simplified function, ask:

1. **What does this job in the physical world?** (A lighthouse watches for ships. A sentry watches a gate.)
2. **What does this in nature?** (An owl hunts at night. A spider sits in a web waiting.)
3. **What tool or machine does an analogous job?** (Radar scans for objects. A sieve filters material.)
4. **What role or profession does this?** (A herald announces news. A curator selects what matters.)
5. **What's the dramatic/extreme version of this function?** (A canary dies to warn miners. A tripwire triggers an alarm.)
6. **What's the quiet/subtle version?** (An undercurrent moves beneath the surface. A root network transfers nutrients underground.)

### Step 3: Map Territories

Don't rush to individual names. Map out **conceptual territories** — clusters of related metaphors. Pick the 2-3 richest territories, then generate names within them.

### Step 4: Cross-Pollinate

The most distinctive names often come from combining elements of two different territories. A monitoring product doesn't have to use "watcher" words — it could borrow from the natural world (Owl, Canary) or from architecture (Watchtower, Bastion) or from music (Monitor itself comes from audio monitoring).

## Cross-Pollination Examples

The richest names often come from unexpected territory combinations:

| Product | Territories crossed | Result concept |
| --------- | --------------------- | ---------------- |
| Error monitoring | Military + Mining | Canary (coal mine warning system applied to code) |
| Container orchestration | Nautical + Computing | Helm (steering a ship → steering containers) |
| CI/CD pipeline | Metalworking + Computing | Forge (raw materials → finished product) |
| Log aggregation | Textile + Data | Loom (weaving threads together) |
| Database | Geology + Storage | Bedrock, Obsidian, Slate |

**The pattern:** Take the function from one territory and the imagery from another. "Error monitoring" is security/detection territory, but borrowing from mining (canary) produces a more vivid and specific name than staying in the security territory (guard, watch, sentinel — all overused).
